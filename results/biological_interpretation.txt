Human PPI Network Analysis - Biological Interpretation
=======================================================

1. Degree of every node
The degree of a protein is the number of other proteins it directly interacts with.
Proteins with a high degree are called "hubs" (e.g. TP53, EGFR) and are often
essential proteins involved in core biological processes. Most proteins in the
network have a low degree (few interactions), while a small number of hubs have
very high degrees -- this is a key feature of biological networks.

2. Clustering Coefficient
The clustering coefficient measures how connected a protein's neighbours are to
each other. A high clustering coefficient means the protein sits inside a tight
functional module or protein complex. The average clustering coefficient of this
network is 0.2098, meaning proteins tend to form moderately tight local clusters,
which often correspond to biological pathways or complexes.

3. Average Clustering Coefficient (0.2098)
This value tells us the network is moderately modular. Proteins do not interact
randomly -- they tend to group into functional communities, which reflects the
organised nature of cellular biology (e.g. signalling pathways, ribosomes,
splicing complexes).

4. Scale-Free Structure (gamma = 1.56, R2 = 0.851)
The degree distribution follows an approximate power law, meaning the network is
roughly scale-free. True scale-free networks have gamma between 2 and 3; our
value of 1.56 is slightly below this, likely because curated PPI databases
over-represent well-studied hub proteins. Biologically, scale-free structure means
the network is robust to random protein loss (e.g. random mutations) but vulnerable
to targeted removal of hub proteins -- which is why hub proteins are often essential
genes and good drug targets.

5. Shortest Path Lengths
The mean shortest path between proteins in both sets is around 2.1-2.2, meaning
any two proteins are on average only about 2 interactions apart. This "small-world"
property means signals and information can travel very efficiently across the
interaction network, which is important for fast cellular responses.

6. Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test (p = 0.4880)
The test found no significant difference in path length distributions between
Protein Set 1 and Protein Set 2 (p = 0.4880, p > 0.05). This means both protein
sets are similarly positioned within the human PPI network -- neither set is more
or less connected to the rest of the network than the other. Biologically, this
suggests both sets of proteins operate within similarly accessible regions of the
interactome, with no evidence that one group is more centralised than the other.
